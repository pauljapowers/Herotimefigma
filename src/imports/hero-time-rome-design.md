Design a responsive web app and website called “Hero Time! – Rome”, the first world in a larger Hero Time! series that will eventually include many ancient civilizations. The primary audience is children aged 7–11 (KS2) and their teachers/parents.

The app should feel like a game first, with strong educational value underneath: history, myths, reading, maths (including Roman numerals), science and engineering, and critical thinking. It must work well on tablets, laptops, and interactive whiteboards.

Play (Games & Tools) – Rome
Design a grid of game/tool cards with clear icons, difficulty labels, and estimated time (e.g., 5 min, 10 min). Each card has Play button, star rating (difficulty), and topic tags.

Key interactive tools and games to design layouts for:

Roman Numeral Lab

Converter: input box for Arabic numbers, output in big Roman numerals and vice versa.

Interactive numeral “blocks” (I, V, X, L, C, D, M) pupils can drag to build numbers.

Difficulty presets: beginner (1–20), intermediate (1–100), advanced (up to 3999).

Instant feedback: green/red highlighting, hints, and “Try again” states.

Timeline Builder

Horizontal timeline of Roman history with draggable events (founding of Rome, Republic start, Caesar, empire peak, fall of Rome, etc.).

Pupils drag event cards into the correct order and get feedback plus mini‑explanations.

Story Path Game – A Day in Rome

Choose‑your‑own‑adventure layout with illustrated panels and choices.

Reading comprehension questions built into the story; answering correctly unlocks the next path.

Engineering Lab

Simple interactive simulations mock‑up (e.g., how an aqueduct works, building a straight Roman road).

Sliders or toggles that change variables (slope, material, etc.) with explanatory text.

Myth Match

Matching game for gods, symbols, and myths (e.g., Jupiter – thunderbolt).

Card‑matching grid, timer, and streak display.

Design, interactivity, and gamification
Make it feel like a game launcher rather than a static website.

Use bright, adventurous Rome‑inspired visuals (columns, laurel wreaths, shields) but avoid gore or realistic violence.

Clear, chunky buttons suitable for touchscreens, with hover/tap states.

Consistent feedback: sounds or visual cues for correct/incorrect answers, level‑ups, and completed quests.

Accessible typography (large, readable fonts) and colour contrast appropriate for KS2 and diverse learners.
